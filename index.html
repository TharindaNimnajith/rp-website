Literature Review
Since English has become the most widely used language in the world, most people are involved in learning English daily as beginners. With the increasing demand, more research has been conducted on learning English language in the e-learning domain recently with little success. With the advancement of computer power and multimedia applications, there has been a new interest in Computer-Assisted Pronunciation Teaching (CAPT) applications in recent years. Pronunciation errors could be classified into two main types called phonemic and prosodic errors. Those errors have different features. By extracting those features, software can measure the pronunciation level of a speaker in different ways. Much research is done on pronunciation error detection using these features in various ways. The likelihood-based scoring feature initially used in the 1990s saw the creation of several likelihood-based phoneme-level error detection algorithms. For example, Yoon Kim, Horacio Franco, and Leonardo Neumeyer used log-likelihood scores in forced alignment mode. Expanded versions of likelihood-based scores were also successfully used by Brian Mak et al. Prosodic pronunciation error detection and feedback are very detailed discussions of CAPT systems. Jared Bernstein, Jian Cheng, and Masanori Suzuki have shown that there appears to be a linear relationship between fluency measures and human judgments of proficiency. Pieter Mueller, Febe de Wet, Christa van der Walt, and Thomas Niesler applied phoneme-level log-likelihood scoring to speech from near-native language learners. It was found that the GOP score did not correlate with human ratings. Based on the existing research, there are challenges that need to be overcome to be able to develop a truly useful pronunciation teaching application. In the past few years, the e-learning industry grew rapidly. However, current e-learning industry is mainly focused on the content delivered to students. Furthermore, they deliver the same content to every user, whereas, users are different from each other in terms of their strengths and weaknesses. Much research addresses this one-size-fits-all issue. For example, Schiaffino, Garcia, and Amandi proposed an intelligent agent-eTeacher by victimization Bayesian network technique which provides personalized assistance to e-learning students by observing the behavior of the student while he or she is taking online courses and automatically building the studentâ€™s profile. Halawa, Hamed, and Shehab proposed a personalized e-learning recommendation model based on the psychological type and learning style model focused on student personality types and the dominant preferences based on the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI) theory. Yanni and Ping, the authors of a study on personality learning in e-learning systems have implemented a recommendation engine based on trust value. The intelligent recommendation engine with user interest would help to introduce customized user attraction with the method, according to the above-mentioned values. However, only a limited number of research focuses on user flaws and feedback to incorporate a customized course content recommendation engine. Automated essay evaluation is a trending topic at present research. The beginning of automated essay evaluation was found in the works of Page in the 1960s. In 1966, Page has proposed Project Essay Grader (PEG) which is a system developed to make the essay evaluation process more practical. PEG system identifies a group of text features and proxies, that are considered approximations of the intrinsic variables of writing quality. However, it is only relying on linguistic features where neither NLP nor lexical content is considered [20, 21]. Intelligent Essay Assessor (IEA) has been developed in the late 1990s, for automated essay evaluation, using Latent Semantic Analysis (LSA). Furthermore, Mahana, Johns, and Apte of Stanford University suggested a solution for automated essay grading with linear regression techniques which evaluated the essays based on different parameters such as dexterity, diction, vocabulary, structure, and organization. Even though there are several pieces of research done on automated essay grading, they consist of many limitations such as lower accuracy, dismissing grammatical and spelling mistakes, and lack of proper feedback. The semantic textual similarity is a heavily discussed topic in computational linguistics due to the advancements of AI-based technologies and its heavy usage in multiple domains including the automatic short descriptive answer evaluation process. The difficulty of measuring up to how much the contextual meaning of a text is reflected in another text and the difficulty of accurately computing the semantic textual similarity between two texts are proven in many previously conducted computational linguistics research. Similar study has been conducted on evaluating the paraphrasing of explanation of a graph by Senevirathne, Fernando, White, Divyanjala, Udara Samaratunge, and Dias, where a smart personal intelligent assistant is built for IELTS examination candidates. The above study evaluated the similarity of the sentences using the cosine proximity technique, and Doc2Vec model. The limitation of the sentence similarity module of this system is its low accuracy of 67 percent. Many approaches with BLEU, n-grams, and WordNet based on NLP-based techniques have many shortcomings and limitations such as lower accuracy due to measuring syntactic similarity instead of semantic similarity. Further, in the past few years, there has also been an uptake of expressing ontologies using semantic web languages such as the Web Ontology Language (OWL) for semantic textual similarity evaluation. In conclusion, the majority of research has been conducted by measuring text similarity using various traditional NLP-based methodologies and algorithms for keyword matching. Most of these methods provide less accurate results as they measure the number of matching keywords present in each text and the syntactic similarity, rather than the semantic similarity. Due to the high complexities of linguistics in the English language in general, it is not possible to claim the two texts are similar in meaning just by matching keywords, without trying to understand the overall meaning and semantics of the text. But the state-of-the-art methodologies using deep learning and neural networks try to compare two texts based on their overall meaning and provide a more reliable and accurate similarity evaluation. In conclusion, most of these previous research and existing systems have major shortcomings and limitations. Thus, to overcome the above-given limitations the proposed system includes several novel features.
Research Problem
Most Sri Lankans struggle with English examinations to reach expected grades that are required for their educational and career goals. The English Language literacy in Sri Lanka is just 22% among Sri Lanka's population above 15 years of age [1]. There are no recommendation systems for English Language learning applications in Sri Lanka that recommend lessons and activities based on the weaknesses of the particular user in a personalized way [2]. Existing solutions do not provide accurate automatic evaluations for speaking and writing activities with subjective answers such as essays and summarizations based on different criteria focused on grading standards used in globally recognized English language examinations [3]. Objectives
  • Main Objective: Improving the English speaking and academic writing skills among non-native English speakers worldwide by providing comprehensive training with self-learning techniques to increase the performance in English language examinations.
  • Sub Objective 1: Using natural language processing and machine learning to find English pronunciation level and smart calling system to improve student's English speaking level
  • Sub Objective 2: Automated English essay activities evaluation process with detecting formality, fluency, lexical complexity, and spelling/grammatical mistakes
  • Sub Objective 3: Evaluating the relevancy of short descriptive answers by measuring the semantic similarity between the submitted answer and the marking scheme
  • Sub Objective 4: Personalized lesson and activity recommendations using machine learning
